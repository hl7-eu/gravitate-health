1. What Bisoprolol is and what it is used for

Bisoprolol belongs to a group of medicines called beta-blockers. These medicines work by affecting the body’s response to some nerve impulses, especially in the heart. As a result, bisoprolol slows down the heart rate and makes the heart more efficient at pumping blood around the body.

Bisoprolol is used to treat stable chronic heart failure. It is used in combination with other medicines suitable for this condition (such as ACE-inhibitors, diuretics, and heart glycosides).

2. What you need to know before you take Bisoprolol

Do not take Bisoprolol if you:

  • are allergic (hypersensitive) to bisoprolol or any of the other ingredients (see section 6)
  • have severe asthma
  • have severe blood circulation problems in your limbs (e.g. Raynaud’s syndrome)
  • have untreated pheochromocytoma (a tumour of the adrenal gland)
  • have metabolic acidosis (too much acid in the blood)
  • have acute or worsening heart failure requiring IV inotropes
  • have a very slow or irregular heartbeat, low blood pressure or cardiogenic shock

Warnings and precautions

Tell your doctor before taking Bisoprolol if you have:

  • diabetes
  • strict fasting
  • certain heart rhythm disorders or severe chest pain at rest (Prinzmetal’s angina)
  • kidney or liver problems
  • mild blood circulation problems in your limbs
  • chronic lung disease or mild asthma
  • psoriasis or a history of scaly skin rash
  • pheochromocytoma
  • thyroid disorder

Also tell your doctor if you are going to have:

  • desensitization therapy (e.g. for hay fever)
  • anaesthesia (e.g. for surgery)

Children and adolescents

Bisoprolol is not recommended for use in children or adolescents.

Other medicines and Bisoprolol

Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking, have recently taken or might take any other medicines. In particular, do not take without medical advice:

  • Class I antiarrhythmics (quinidine, disopyramide, lidocaine, phenytoin, flecainide, propafenone)
  • Verapamil, diltiazem
  • Clonidine, methyldopa, moxonidine, rilmenidine

And consult your doctor before taking:

  • Dihydropyridine-type calcium antagonists (felodipine, amlodipine)
  • Amiodarone
  • Topical beta-blockers (timolol eye drops)
  • Parasympathomimetics (tacrine, carbachol) or sympathomimetics (isoprenaline, dobutamine)
  • Antidiabetics including insulin
  • Digitalis (for heart failure)
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac)
  • Any medicine that lowers blood pressure (e.g. antihypertensives, tricyclic antidepressants, barbiturates, phenothiazines)
  • Mefloquine (antimalarial)
  • MAO inhibitors (except MAO-B inhibitors)

Pregnancy and breast-feeding

If you are pregnant or breast-feeding, think you may be pregnant or are planning to have a baby, ask your doctor or pharmacist for advice before taking this medicine.

Pregnancy

There is a risk that bisoprolol may harm the baby. Your doctor will decide whether you can take it during pregnancy.

Breast-feeding

It is not known whether bisoprolol passes into human breast milk. Breast-feeding is not recommended during therapy.

Driving and using machines

Your ability to drive or use machinery may be affected, especially at the start of treatment, after a dose increase or combined with alcohol.

3. How to take Bisoprolol

Always take this medicine exactly as your doctor has told you. Take the tablet with water in the morning, with or without food. Do not crush or chew the tablet. Scored tablets can be divided into equal doses.

Adults including the elderly

Treatment must start at a low dose and be increased gradually as follows:

  • 1.25 mg once daily for 1 week
  • 2.5 mg once daily for 1 week
  • 3.75 mg once daily for 1 week
  • 5 mg once daily for 4 weeks
  • 7.5 mg once daily for 4 weeks
  • 10 mg once daily for maintenance

The maximum recommended daily dose is 10 mg. If you stop treatment, your doctor will advise you how to taper off.

If you take more Bisoprolol than you should

Tell your doctor immediately. Symptoms of overdose may include very slow heart rate, severe breathing difficulty, dizziness or trembling.

If you forget to take Bisoprolol

Do not take a double dose to make up for a forgotten dose. Take your usual dose the next morning.

If you stop taking Bisoprolol

Never stop without your doctor’s advice, as your condition could worsen.

4. Possible side effects

Like all medicines, this medicine can cause side effects, although not everybody gets them.

Serious side effects

The most serious side effects are related to heart function:

  • Slowing of heart rate (may affect more than 1 in 10 people)
  • Worsening of heart failure (may affect up to 1 in 10 people)
  • Slow or irregular heartbeat (may affect up to 1 in 100 people)

If you feel dizzy, weak or have breathing difficulties, contact your doctor as soon as possible.

Further side effects are listed below by frequency:

Common (may affect up to 1 in 10 people)

  • Tiredness, weakness, dizziness, headache
  • Coldness or numbness in hands or feet
  • Low blood pressure
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, constipation

Uncommon (may affect up to 1 in 100 people)

  • Sleep disturbances, depression
  • Dizziness on standing
  • Breathing problems in asthma or chronic lung disease
  • Muscle weakness, cramps

Rare (may affect up to 1 in 1,000 people)

  • Hearing problems, allergic rhinitis
  • Liver inflammation (jaundice), abnormal liver or fat tests
  • Severe allergic reactions (swelling of face, tongue, throat)
  • Impaired erection, nightmares, hallucinations, fainting

Very rare (may affect up to 1 in 10,000 people)

  • Conjunctivitis, hair loss, psoriasis-like rash

Reporting of side effects

If you get any side effects, talk to your doctor or pharmacist. You can also report side effects via the Yellow Card Scheme at www.mhra.gov.uk/yellowcard.

5. How to store Bisoprolol

  • Keep this medicine out of the sight and reach of children.
  • Do not use this medicine after the expiry date on the blister or carton.
  • This medicinal product does not require any special storage conditions.
  • Do not dispose via wastewater or household waste. Ask your pharmacist how to throw away medicines you no longer use.

6. Contents of the pack and other information

What Bisoprolol contains

  • Bisoprolol 1.25 mg film-coated tablets:
    • Active substance: bisoprolol fumarate, 1.25 mg per tablet.
    • Other ingredients: microcrystalline cellulose, calcium hydrogen phosphate, pregelatinised starch, crospovidone, colloidal anhydrous silica, magnesium stearate; film coating: hypromellose (E 464), titanium dioxide (E 171), macrogols.
  • Bisoprolol 2.5 mg film-coated tablets:
    • Active substance: bisoprolol fumarate, 2.5 mg per tablet.
    • Other ingredients: as above.
  • Bisoprolol 3.75 mg film-coated tablets:
    • Active substance: bisoprolol fumarate, 3.75 mg per tablet.
    • Other ingredients: as above plus iron oxide yellow (E 172).
  • Bisoprolol 7.5 mg film-coated tablets:
    • Active substance: bisoprolol fumarate, 7.5 mg per tablet.
    • Other ingredients: as above plus iron oxide yellow (E 172) and iron oxide red (E 172).

What Bisoprolol looks like and pack sizes

  • 1.25 mg: white to off-white, round, biconvex tablets, 5 mm, debossed “C”/“42”. Packs of 10, 20, 28 (Alu-Alu) or 30, 100, 300, 1 000 (HDPE).
  • 2.5 mg: white to off-white, round, 7 mm, debossed “C”/deep notch/“41”. Packs of 10, 28 (Alu-Alu) or 30, 32, 100, 105, 300, 1 000 (HDPE).
  • 3.75 mg: off-white, round, 8 mm, debossed “C”/deep notch/“40”. Packs of 10, 28 (Alu-Alu) or 30, 100, 1 000 (HDPE).
  • 7.5 mg: pale-yellow, round, 8 mm, debossed “C”/deep notch/“38”. Packs of 10, 28 (Alu-Alu) or 30, 100, 1 000 (HDPE).
  • Not all pack sizes may be marketed.
